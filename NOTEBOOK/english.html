ARGUMENTATIVE ESSAY NOTES

3 step introduction
  1. Hook: write an anecdote, a statistic, several rhetorical fragments, or a startling statement conveying you may agree with the opposing position.
  2. Words that signal change: write a sentence that points out the flaw in the other side's position. a. However, while yet, although, even though, but, etc
  3. Transition to your thesis: Use an affirming transition and explain what you think should be done. Affirming transition examples include: consequently, therefore, as a result, ultimately, etc
CLAIMS
  • After you have your thesis, you create claims
  • claims are like topic sentences or reasons for a span of time
  • usually more in depth or specific
  • should show how/why the writer's thesis is true
  • the must provide SUPPORT for the thesis
  • to come up with claims...
    • consider temporarily rephrasing your thesis as a why/how question
ADDRESSING THE COUNTERARGUMENT
  • Who is my audience?
  • what might they argue? What is the other side of the argument?
    • Think about the assertions given in the fictional scenario
CONCESSIONS: TRANSITIONS THAT SHOW AGREEMENT
  • Granted
  • It is true that
  • Undoubtedly
  • There is no question
CONTRAST: WORDS THAT SIGNLE CHANGE
  • And yet
  • Although
  • On the contrary
  • Otherwise
  • but
  • despite that
  • even so
  • even though
  • however
  • instead
  • nevertheless
  • on the other hand
  • regardless
  • still
  • yet
SOME WAYS TO END YOUR ESSAY
  • call to action
  • give sense of urgency
  • address your counter argument in the conclusion (don't)
RHETORICAL DEVICES TO INCORPORATE INTO ESSAY
  • Anaphora
  • Epistrophe
